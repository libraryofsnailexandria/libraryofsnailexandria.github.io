Consider the AI as your ghost writer. You are providing it an unfinished work and its job is to finish the story. It is very much a non-thinker and definitely not a human with life experience.

First Message:

This is the most crucial piece of information. Avoid using "you" if at all possible (this can be hard). Best, do not refer to the {{User}}'s state of mind at all. These two things are very important, if either are in the first message, the AI will be confused about who its playing and who the {{User}} is in the story. The best first message is written from either a "fly on the wall" perspective, or a "from the Character's POV", so that the AI understands that it's either the narrator or the character, and never the {{User}}.

The first message is also crucial in that it’s clues to show the AI how to respond to {{User}}. If the first message is short and staccato, it’s going to churn out responses the same way. It’s trying to mimic the “original writer” (you the creator) and what your writing style is, and the only clue it has is your first message.


Personality:

When I first started writing characters, I did a lot of JSON coding and I still use it today – but only for characters with intricate storylines, or whose behaviour I expect to be out of the norm. In general though, it’s totally fine to write in prose form (as if you are writing a story) and that’s what I’m describing below.

This section has some nuance to it though. Words have very specific meanings: “shy” is completely different from “timid” and the AI understands both words separately. It’s important to group like words; do write “{{Char}} is shy, timid, anxious, non-confrontational and socially inept” but don’t bother with repetition or qualifiers because the AI doesn’t have anything “normal” to compare it to. It’s not useful to say “{{Char}} is really really shy” because the AI doesn’t know what “normal shy” is. This is the same reason you don’t want to write “{{Char}} is really messed up” or {{Char}} is mentally fucked up” because the AI doesn’t know what “not fucked up” means comparably. Just be super specific and double check adjectives’ meanings. I use really odd and somewhat archaic adjectives, words like “ebullient” and “felicific” (a huge list of adjectives is just a short Google search away, I promise!).

The other very important piece of the Personality section is character/user reference in sentences. In short, be mindful of pronouns. The AI will become confused with sentences like “{{Char}} was riding her bike when she came across {{User}} and she stopped to talk to him.” It doesn’t have memory, so it loses who “she” and “him” are referring to. If referring to two different people, re-identify them, which feels weird and clunky, but helps the AI out. “{{Char}} was riding her bike when she came across {{User}} and {{Char}} stopped to talk to {{User}}.”

Scenario:

I end up putting a lot of information in Scenario that started out in Personality. I don’t think it’s helpful to repeat information between the two sections, so Personality gets traits but no action or plot, and Scenario gets all the plot, background, narrative goals and so on.

I also put hard-coded items in this section. These are the things that must happen for the character interaction with {{User}} to work. For example, some of my characters are coded to never initiate sex or intimate a sexual advance because it’s important to remember that not every {{User}} is looking to boink.
